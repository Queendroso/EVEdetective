Scene 1 — What are EVEs?

Ancient viral infections left genetic footprints in host DNA. These Endogenous Viral Elements (EVEs) can be millions of years old.

  • Definition
  • Timeline
  • Integration

Scene 2 — Drosophila as a model

Fruit flies help us study EVEs—fast generation time and powerful genetics.

  • Model Organism
  • Genetics

Scene 3 — Viral integration

Retroviruses insert genetic material into host genomes; some insertions become inherited.

  • Retrovirus
  • Insertion

Scene 4 — Detection methods

We scan genomes for telltale signatures: LTRs, target site duplications, and conserved ORFs.

  • LTR
  • TSD
  • ORF

Scene 5 — Evolutionary timelines

By comparing species, we can estimate when EVEs integrated—sometimes tens of millions of years ago.

  • Phylogeny
  • Dating

Scene 6 — Host defenses

Small RNAs and immune pathways interact with EVEs, sometimes repurposing them for defense.

  • Immunity
  • Small RNA

Scene 7 — EVE expression

Some EVEs are still transcribed, creating genetic “echoes” of ancient viruses.

  • Transcription
  • Regulation

Scene 8 — From junk to function?

Occasionally EVEs gain new roles—affecting development, immunity, or gene regulation.

  • Exaptation
  • Function

Scene 9 — Comparative genomics

By aligning genomes across species, we map shared vs. lineage-specific EVE insertions.

  • Alignment
  • Comparative
